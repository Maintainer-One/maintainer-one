# Maintainer One: Game Lore

## Introduction: The Founding Era

Maintainer One is still in its infancy, an era early participants call the
**Founding Era**.

The League was not born in a stadium or a television studio. It began as a
shared open-source experiment among software maintainers, robotics builders,
simulation obsessives, and people who wanted a sport where the real competition
was the system behind the team.

The first arenas were not grand venues. They were controlled test beds in
retrofitted warehouses and industrial spaces: marked floors, calibrated target
pads, cheap cameras, and enough safety barriers to keep the machines from
turning a weekend hack session into a very expensive incident report.

What started as late-night tooling, local simulations, and warehouse scrimmages
has become the first official Maintainer One League.

## The Maintainers

The title **Maintainer** comes from open-source software. A Team Maintainer is
not a coach with a joystick and not a player on the field. A maintainer is the
person responsible for the logic, tools, tests, and operating philosophy that
drive an autonomous team.

In the early era, that means writing and improving match logic. Over time, it
will mean maintaining the automated organization itself: draft logic, recovery
logic, trade logic, scouting logic, deployment logic, and the systems that help a
team adapt to each new protocol.

Maintainers do not manually move bots, approve trades, or pick draft targets in
the moment. They build the systems that make those decisions when the official
phase runs.

## Contributors

Maintainer One teams are not meant to be one-person mysteries forever. Each
official team can have a small, trusted group of maintainers and a broader group
of contributors who submit code, analysis, tests, tooling, scouting reports,
lore, or strategic ideas.

Contributors may never hold the keys to an official team, but they can still
shape its history. The best contributions are the ones that survive contact with
the league: adopted logic, useful tests, accurate scouting, sharp postmortems,
and tools that help a team understand itself.

## Protocols

The League is governed by versioned protocols. A protocol defines the sport for
an era: what the bots can do, what the arena measures, what information teams
receive, and which automated decisions matter.

Protocol versions are not only balance patches. They are a learning ladder.
Lower versions teach the foundational problems directly. Later versions can add
new capabilities, new constraints, and new phases after the old problems have
become familiar.

The official league may move forward, but older protocols remain part of the
world. Private leagues, test leagues, and local servers can freeze on the
versions they enjoy.

## Protocol V1: Capture the Core

**Protocol V1** is the first official standardized ruleset.

### The Arena

The current arenas are gritty, controlled test beds. The floors are marked with
a discrete grid. The scoring pads are calibrated target zones. The broadcast is
less spectacle than instrumentation: enough to see what happened, replay it, and
argue about why.

### The Bots

The inaugural bots are uniform prototype chassis. They are stripped down to the
bare essentials required to execute movement decisions. No advanced components,
no specialized roles, no hidden traits.

They are intentionally simple because V1 is not the forever sport. V1 is where
maintainers learn movement, distance, timing, collision, zone control, replay
analysis, and the discipline of writing logic that can be inspected.

### The Objective

"Capture the Core" is the League's first functional test of autonomous team
logic. The Core is not a supernatural prize. It is a calibrated point zone used
to test whether a team can navigate physical space, control opportunity, and
outperform hostile logic under deterministic rules.

## The Inaugural Teams

The first official season begins with six founding teams: **Amber, Beige,
Crimson, Denim, Emerald, and Fuchsia**.

In the Founding Era, these teams are closer to test-bed franchises than corporate
giants. Their identities are simple, but that simplicity is part of the point.
They are blank slates waiting to accumulate history: bitter losses, strange
strategies, beloved bots, bad drafts, unlikely recoveries, and maintainers who
leave fingerprints on the league.

The top official league is meant to be small and prestigious. Maintainer One is
also open-source by nature, so anyone can eventually run private leagues with
their own friends, hardware, hosting, protocol preferences, and house history.

## Beyond Matches

As the protocols evolve, the league will become more than a sequence of games.
Every official phase can become part of the sport.

Future seasons may include:

- **Scouting phases** where teams spend limited resources to learn about draft
  pools, components, opponents, or hidden traits.
- **Draft phases** where team logic evaluates and selects upgrades, chassis, or
  organizational systems.
- **Recovery phases** where teams decide how to spend limited maintenance on
  bots carrying wear from previous matches.
- **Trade windows** where automated trade logic proposes, evaluates, counters,
  and accepts asset exchanges.
- **Prep phases** where teams tune strategy packages for a specific matchup.

The same rule applies everywhere: if a phase matters, it should be replayable,
inspectable, and capable of producing artifacts the world remembers.

## Future Systems

The bots will not stay uniform forever. Later protocols will introduce new
components, chassis families, reliability traits, wear, sensors, mobility
systems, and compatibility tradeoffs.

The front office will evolve too. Staff may be represented less as humans and
more as organizational systems: scouting arrays, repair rigs, analytics stacks,
coaching modules, research benches, and trade evaluators. These systems will
have their own strengths, weaknesses, and long-term consequences.

The mature version of a team is not just `players + code`. It is bots,
components, staff systems, telemetry, history, constraints, and the maintained
logic that decides how all of it should act.

The Founding Era ends when the league outgrows its origins. As the protocols
mature and the community grows, the league will eventually become popular enough
that robotics manufacturers take notice. When they arrive — pouring real money
in and developing advanced hardware specifically for the league — the uniform
prototype chassis of the early era are retired and something larger begins.

## Public Code and Closed Garages

In the Founding Era, public logic is a feature. It lets anyone learn, test
locally, replay matches, and contribute. Open code creates a shared strategic
commons where teams can study each other and still diverge as protocols add
personnel, components, and context.

If the top league ever becomes large enough for privacy and compute limits to
matter, later formats may develop closed garages: private team logic, official
simulation budgets, and stricter audit requirements. That future is not needed
for the early sport, but the world leaves room for it.

For now, it is still the Founding Era: your team, your logic, the official seed,
and a small world beginning to remember what happened.
