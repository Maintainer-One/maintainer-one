# Maintainer One: Game Lore

## Introduction: The "Garage Era"

The world of **Maintainer One** is currently in its infancy—an era referred to by early pioneers as the "Garage Era." 

The League was not born in a massive, multi-billion dollar stadium. It started as an underground passion project by a coalition of wealthy tech enthusiasts, eccentric robotics engineers, and AI researchers. Seeking a way to test the absolute limits of autonomous robotics and deterministic logic, they pooled their resources to create a physical proving ground.

What started as late-night hacking sessions and weekend tech demos in retrofitted industrial warehouses has slowly evolved into a formalized competition: The Maintainer One League.

## The Maintainers

In this foundational era, the title of **Maintainer** holds deep roots in the open-source software movement. The foundational engine and physics models that power the bots were originally an open-source repository. 

A "Team Maintainer" is literally someone who maintains a proprietary fork of that original open-source logic. You aren't a traditional sports coach or a player with a joystick; you are an elite software engineer. Your job is to push commits to your team's logic repository, attempting to out-code and out-engineer the other forks on the grid. 

## Protocol V1: Capture the Core

Because the league is in its infancy, the technology is raw. **Protocol V1** is the very first official, standardized rule-set. 

### The Arena
The current arenas are not pristine eSports stadiums. They are gritty, highly-controlled test-beds built inside abandoned hangars and warehouses. The floors are scuffed concrete marked with standardized grid tape. 

### The Bots
The inaugural bots are bulky, clunky prototypes. They are essentially moving chassis boxes stripped down to the bare essentials required to execute movement vectors. They have no weapons, no advanced sensors, and no specialized "speed" parts. They are perfectly uniform blocks of metal and circuitry.

### The Objective
"Capture the Core" is simply the League's first functional test of autonomous logic. "The Core" isn't a magical energy source—it's just a designated, highly-calibrated target pad (a "point zone") used to measure a bot's ability to navigate and control physical space against a hostile logic script.

## The Inaugural Factions (ABCDEF)

To bootstrap the first official season, the Founding Board created six generic, test-bed franchises: **Amber, Beige, Crimson, Denim, Emerald, and Fuchsia.** 

These are not yet the branded, corporate juggernauts of a mature sports league. They are simply standardized factory designations—blank slates waiting for sponsors to eventually buy them out and rebrand them. 

## The Future: Evolution and Quirks

The founders know that perfectly deterministic machines playing a perfectly solved game will eventually become stale. As the League grows in popularity and attracts the attention of massive robotics and software corporations, the protocols will evolve.

Rumors are already circulating among the Founding Board about future regulations (V2, V50, and beyond):
- **Corporate Takeovers:** The generic color teams will eventually be purchased by corporate sponsors, turning the warehouse hobby into a global spectacle hosted in massive arenas.
- **The Upgrade Draft:** As chassis technology advances, bots will age and undergo metal fatigue. Maintainers will have to navigate a complex B2B economy to draft new bots or salvage "Speed Modules" and hardware components to bolt onto their aging roster.
- **Mandated "Quirks":** To prevent the sport from becoming a sterile execution of perfect code, the League Authority plans to mandate that manufacturers intentionally inject "Quirks" (purpose-built deficiencies) into the bots. A bot might have a glitchy thruster or a misaligned sensor. The true test of a Maintainer will be writing the resilient logic needed to handle the imperfect, "human-like" fallibility of their physical machines.
- **Algorithmic General Managers:** Eventually, the entire franchise—from scouting parts to managing budgets—will be handled via the League's robust supply chain APIs, turning the sport into a battle of fully automated corporate empires.

For now, however, it's just you, your logic repository, and Protocol V1. Welcome to the Garage Era.
